    “It was devastating, I became pregnant when I was 18 out of wedlock and ended up getting married," she told Women Power Africa."I didn’t know how I was going to ever fulfil my dream. It was sad because that was the one time I saw my dad cry," she added.

    According to the medic, she got married because she thought it was the best thing to do then.

    Being involved in an unplanned marriage, Matemavi ended up enduring physical, mental and emotional abuse.

    Working as a nurse to raise fees

    She got divorced at the age of 23, with four-year-old and two-year-old children.

    As a single mother of two, she worked as a cardiac nurse to put herself through medical school, and she was the only woman in her residency class.

    It was not her preference, but it would provide a steady stream of income to pay for school because she was not a US citizen at the time and therefore did not have access to any financial aid.

    She fought her way to the top in a male-dominated field despite hearing discouraging comments like, "girls don't belong in an operating room," from some attending physicians.

    Against all odds, Matemavi obtained a Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine from Michigan State College of Osteopathic Medicine.

    "My faith in God and my belief in myself kept me going through the difficult times. Giving up on my dream was not an option," she said.

    Graduates as surgeon

    She did her general surgery residency at New York Presbyterian Queens Hospital and completed her Multivisceral Transplant Surgery Fellowship at the University of Nebraska Medical Centre.

    Matemavi is currently an Assistant Professor of Surgery at the University of Mississippi Medical Center and a published author of 'Passion and Purpose: Black Female Surgeons'.

    "There are times when it was hard to even get out of bed, but I kept going. One foot in front of the other, one moment at a time, one hour at a time, one day at a time," she said. "The storms in life do not last forever, remember that," added Matemavi.

    While in medical school, Matemavi met and married a fellow medical student believing she had finally found someone who understood her demanding schedule.

    But by the time she began her surgical residency, there was very little time to spend with her husband.

    The more hectic her schedule became, the more Matemavi learned to depend on God to make it through each day. But unfortunately, the opposite happened with her husband, and the two grew apart.

    Kenya's first female radio pharmacists

    In a related story, TUKO.co.k reported Radiopharmaceuticals are a key component of nuclear medicine and are crucial to fighting cancer and several other medical conditions.

    Their use, however, requires extensive personnel training on patient safety and equipment handling; they contain small amounts of radioactive isotopes.

    They are used to diagnose and treat diseases and need to be produced under carefully controlled conditions and tested for quality before being given to patients.

    However, there are countable radio pharmacists across the continent and only two ore in Kenya.

    Elizabeth Itotia is one of them and what's even more exciting is that she is the first female radio pharmacist in the country, and she couldn't be prouder.

    The down-to-earth specialist works as a pharmacist at the Kenyatta University Teaching, Referral and Research Hospital (KUTRRH).
