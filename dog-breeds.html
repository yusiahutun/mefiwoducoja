    Each month we will be shining a light on different dog breeds in our series, Dog Breed of the Month. Next up, we take a look at gorgeous, intelligent Dachshunds.

    "Dachshunds, especially the Miniature Smooth Haired variety, have become popular in recent years, partially due to their size which makes them suitable for smaller houses and apartments, and also because of their lively and comical nature," Bill Lambert, a Kennel Club spokesperson tells Country Living.

    Is a Dachshund a good family dog?

    Dachshunds are generally good family dogs, although they can have an independent and stubborn side when it comes to training. They are often known for being boisterous when playing and are best suited for older children.

    Shauna Walsh, PDSA vet nurse, tells us: "Dachshunds are affectionate and playful little dogs who can have a lot of energy despite their small size. They love to curl up and burrow into warm places, so after their adventures, they will be looking for a cosy place to sleep."

    How often should you groom a Dachshund?

    Dachshunds will need to be groomed regularly, but this also depends on what type of coat your dog has.

    "Dachshunds have a variety of coat types – smooth, long, or wire-hired – and can be either standard or miniature-sized. Grooming will depend on coat type, with the shorthaired types likely to need weekly brushing, and the longhaired types daily brushing to prevent matting," continues Shauna.

    Are there any health concerns with Dachshunds?

    "Back problems are one of the most common health issues in Dachshunds due to their short legs and long spines. The most common back condition is called intervertebral disc disease [EM1] (IVDD)," Shauna tells us.

    "This condition causes the discs that provide cushioning between the vertebrae in their spine to lose their sponginess, becoming hard and fragile. Sometimes they can slip out of place, which can cause pain, nerve problems, and even paralysis.

    "Because of this increased risk, many Dachshund owners will adjust their pet’s home environment to try to manage this risk. This may include preventing use of stairs, using ramps to prevent jumping on and off furniture, avoiding rough play, and using non-slip flooring."

    Other common health problems in Dachshunds include issues around the eye (in particular progressive retinal atrophy which can cause blindness), obesity, heart disease, and seizures.

    Shauna adds: "When choosing a puppy, look for a breeder who health tests their dogs, and ask about the medical history of the parents, grandparents, and great-grandparents. Due to their potential health conditions, which could be very costly to diagnose and treat, we would recommend getting pet insurance for your Dachshund."

    Are Dachshunds easy to train?

    "Dachshunds have complex personalities with many traits linked to their original breeding purpose – hunting and chasing animals in dark underground tunnels. They are courageous and clever, but their stubbornness can make them harder to train as sometimes they like to make their own rules," Bill tells CL.

    It's worth knowing that they are one of the most stubborn dog breeds, so you may need a little know-how before attempting to train them.

    Can Dachshunds be left alone?

    Like many breeds, you should never leave Dachshunds alone for long periods. As pack animals, they can get bored, anxious, lonely and stressed on their own. If you do have to leave your pup alone, try to see if there is someone who can take care of them.

    "They may be small but they still need mental stimulation and exercise – they are really big dogs in little bodies!"

    Oliver Reid / 500px//Getty Images

    Are Dachshunds naughty?

    As young puppies, some Dachshunds can get into chewing habits but this should wear off as they get older. Commonly, Dachshunds mostly chew when they are bored and lonely. They are very intelligent dogs, so with proper training, they will stop chewing.

    Remember: if you are worried about your dogs' behaviour, consult your local vet for expert advice.

    Where do Dachshunds originate from?

    "This breed originated in Germany where they were bred to hunt rabbits and badgers – hence their name, which means 'badger dog' in German," says Shauna. "Their long bodies, small size, and short legs were particularly useful in flushing out their prey from their underground dens."

    Oliver Reid / 500px//Getty Images

    Do Dachshunds like to cuddle?

    Loyal to their owners, Dachshunds love to snuggle up to their owners. They are also very protective of their owners, so don't be surprised if they end up following you around the house.

    "They are a good choice for experienced owners who are willing to put time and effort into their training. Dachshunds can be protective and will get on well with older children who take care of them and respect their space."

    Are Dachshunds good dogs if you live in a flat?

    Yes, thanks to their miniature size. Bill explains: "Their size makes them suitable for owners with a smaller house or apartment, although standard size Dachshunds require more exercise, with an opportunity to sniff and run around."
