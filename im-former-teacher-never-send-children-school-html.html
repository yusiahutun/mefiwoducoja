    A mom-of-three has divided audiences after claiming that traditional schooling system causes 'educational trauma' — and she doesn't want her kids to suffer as she did.

    Former teacher Taylor Moran, 32, refuses to enroll her children in school, declaring that the educational system stifles their creative function, kills spirit and takes precious time away from other important activities.

    Instead, the mom insists on 'unschooling' her three boys — choosing to teach them at home, swapping classrooms and homework for survival skills, and how to make food from scratch.

    Taylor, from Arkansas, declared that working in the traditional education system doesn't teach kids the right life skills, and she wants her sons — Hudson, five; Thompson, three; and one-year-old Gus — to learn to live practically.

    Mom-of-three Taylor Moran, 32, (pictured) doesn't send her kids to a traditional school - claiming she has 'educational trauma'

    Taylor wanted her sons — Hudson, five; Thompson, three; and one-year-old Gus — to learn to live practically

    'School killed my spirit, delayed my creative function, and took precious time from me – I don't want my children to have the same regrets,' Taylor, a certified ex-elementary school teacher told NeedToKnow.co.uk.

    The mom explained her teaching career 'really never took off,' and she was completing her student teaching portion of her degree when she found out she was pregnant with Hudson in 2017.

    'I saw the system from the inside and I opted out, both as a career option and as an option for my kids,' she recalled.

    Taylor says her and her husband Nick have taught their children the practical skills they'll need in life, saying they've learned to do things like make sourdough and yoghurt, as well as how to catch and boil wild crawfish.

    'A few things my children have learned that they would not have in a traditional schooling environment include how to butcher chickens for meat and use a knife safely,' she explained.

    'They've also learnt how to incubate eggs and hatch baby chicks, and how to brood and care for them, as well as how to identify poison ivy and treat a rash with herbs, how to plant a garden, sow seeds and how to cut down trees,' she said.

    The kids don't just learn 'physical skills' – the parents also teach them how to 'think critically' - with Taylor adding she won't be teaching them things they won't need to apply in real-life scenarios. 

    'We aren't specifically withholding or shielding our kids from any particular topics; though I won't personally be teaching them calculus it doesn't mean they won't learn it if they need it,' she said.

    Another important subject matter to the family is philosophy, which is one subject she insists her kids learn.

    'Both Nick and myself majored in philosophy at the University of North Texas, and Hudson currently takes an Outschool class on this topic (an online platform with Zoom-based lessons),' Taylor explained. 

    'The class sizes are small, often less than five students per class.'

    Taylor also stressed the importance of reading, saying she and her husband encouraged their boys to read a lot.

    'Reading is huge for us — we have lots of books and the boys love reading out loud,' she explained.

    Taylor recalled a conversation about math she had with a fifth-grader when she was a student teacher that encouraged her to 'unschool' her own children.

    '[The fifth-grader] wasn't "doing well" in it, and was having to take an extra class during "intervention hour" to catch up – it was heartbreaking because she was at the top of the class when it came to reading and writing,' she anguished. 

    'The system had her staring at the walls while we drilled her on math problems she didn't care about.

    The mom said from the moment she became a mother she 'knew' she wanted to homeschool them.

    Taylor hit back at people who said the lack of socializing for kids was the number-one concern brought up about homeschooling.

    'And rightfully so,' she added. 'But being assigned to a desk, walking in a straight line, raising your hand to speak, asking for permission to use the bathroom — is that socializing?'

    She continued saying her idea of socialization looks like 'running around in nature or a park with other kids.'

    'Socializing happens in life,' Taylor added.

    The family don't have a strict daily schedule, and they adapt their activities depending on how they feel, or what the weather is like.

    '[In the morning] we wake up and eat breakfast, which is farm-fresh eggs, potatoes, and bell peppers,' she explained.

    Taylor said after breakfast, they typically did some craft and then went outside.

    'We've found that the sooner we get outside, the better our days go,' she explained.

    They spend a lot of time together during the day, enjoying a family lunch, and start an activity as a family – such as building

    Despite negative feedback, Taylor insists she and her husband have found a community of parents 'around the world' who 'are on the same wavelength'

    'I take the kids to the forest playground and set them up with a craft like painting – they paint rocks, pinecones, and sticks,' she continued.

    'I either do yoga or lift weights while they craft.'

    Afterward, the family will do a walk or hike, and later Hudson will do his online class.

    The younger kids will then have a nap and they enjoy a family lunch, and start an activity as a family – such as building.

    Taylor shares her unique education approach on TikTok, admitting she does receive backlash from people doubting her lifestyle.

    In one viral clip, with over 8 million views, she showed her eight-year-old raking the yard because he wanted to learn to 'successfully transplant a variety of native plants without them dying.'

    However thousands commented slamming her lack of curriculum, saying he'll be ill-prepared fort he real world. 

    'I was homeschooled as a kid. Plz don't fantasize about it. It's a terrible thing,' one user wrote.

    'This is glorified educational neglect,' another added.

    'Cool. We're going with reading and addition,' a third user wrote.

    However Taylor insists she and her husband have found a community of parents 'around the world' who 'are on the same wavelength.'

    'Or are at least curious and open-minded about unschooling,' she explained. 

    'At first, I took negative comments personally but then I realized, we have reached a whole new demographic of people.'
