    Manchester girl with epilepsy who died with drugs overdose 'intended to kill herself'

    Rachel Murphy (pictured), 15, had 12 times the recognised fatal dose of morphine when her mother found her body in bed last September A 15-year-old schoolgirl who suffered from epilepsy and back pain has died of a drugs overdose intended to kill her, a coroner ruled today.

    23 Aug 2024, 00:00

    Rachel Murphy (pictured), 15, had 12 times the recognised fatal dose of morphine when her mother found her body in bed last September

    A 15-year-old schoolgirl who suffered from epilepsy and back pain has died of a drugs overdose intended to kill her, a coroner ruled today.

    Rachel Murphy had 12 times the recognised fatal dose of morphine when her mother found her body in bed last September.

    The teenager, who had searched ways to commit suicide on her laptop beforehand, took her father's medication from an unlocked drawer at the family home in Stalybridge, Greater Manchester.

    Her parents Colin and Joann were questioned amid claims they had given her his morphine - which he took for severe athritis - and another powerful painkiller Tramadol in the months before her death.

    But at an inquest in Stockport, Greater Manchester, coroner Joanne Kearsley said Miss Murphy had helped herself to the prescription-only medication.  

    It emerged that the teenager, who told her father she 'wanted to be dead', had suffered stress attacks and low moods.

    She had also been sleeping for long periods at a time and had a history of seizures dating back to 2007.

    Miss Murphy, who was studying at Copley High School in Stalybridge, also suffered from scoliosis and the arthritic condition ankylosing spondylitis.

    Miss Kearsley said she would be filing a report after the inquest was told there were disagreements between doctors, healthcare professionals and family support workers about how best treat Rachel.

    She added that there was a 'fractured and disjointed plan' on how to proceed with Miss Murphy's care and said there was a 'lack of planning.'

    The teenager, who had searched ways to commit suicide on her laptop beforehand, took her father's medication from an unlocked drawer at the family home in Stalybridge, Greater Manchester (pictured, her parents Joann and Colin Murphy outside the inquest) 

    'There is no evidence that on or around the days leading up to her death she was given tramadol or morphine by either of her parents or handed blister packs to keep in her room,' she said.

    'There is no doubt that she knew where the medication was and she had access to it. That no doubt is a burden on you [Miss Murphy's parents] and should act as a warning to all parents.

    'On this basis I am satisfied that at the time Rachel intended in ending her life. At the time she had a number of social and medical issues. This was a very unusual and concerning case.' 

    During the inquest her parents were quizzed by the coroner about the way they medicated their daughter - with advice not to say anything which might incriminate them.

    They had already been questioned by police under caution and admitted giving Miss Murphy the drugs but it is thought no action was taken against them.

    Mrs Murphy, a cleaner, said: 'I'm not hiding anything, so I'm not bothered. I've admitted all of this already.

    It emerged that the teenager, who told her father she 'wanted to be dead', had suffered stress attacks and low moods. She had also been sleeping for long periods at a time and had a history of seizures dating back to 2007

    'In my eyes it was helping my daughter. It was only when she had a really bad fit and really knocked herself about. I know I shouldn't have done it but I did. '

    Mr Murphy - who had been prescribed Tramadol and morphine for his severe arthritis - told the inquest his daughter refused to go the doctor - even though he made appointments for her. 

    He said Miss Murphy told him 'I don't want to be here. I want to be dead' - but added that she did not specifically say she wanted to kill herself.

    When asked about his medicine Mr Murphy, 55, said: 'I had a top drawer with all sorts of stuff in and that's where my morphine was. 

    'If she was going to gets scissors or something she would have seen them. She would have known I was taking them anyway. I was talking about it and if she was within hearing she would hear it.'

    He said he first started giving his daughter Tramadol tablets before switching to morphine when it was prescribed to him by his doctor.

    He added: 'When she had a fit she would fall to her knees, and then on her chest so she would complain that it was sore or painful. 

    Her father said Miss Murphy told him: 'I don't want to be here. I want to be dead' - but added that she did not specifically say she wanted to kill herself

    'It was the only painkillers I had available at the house at that time. It was given when we were out of over the counter painkillers.'

    'To be honest I suspected the painkillers weren't working anyway and I stopped giving her the Tramadol. I just don't believe the Tramadol was particularly helping. 

    'You can build up a tolerance up to them. There was a warning that's been given about her not having my prescription tablets but I thought it was about Tramadol not working. I thought something slightly stronger might.'

    He said he himself had been put on morphine for a month in January 2015 but came off it when it made him sleep too much. 

    He said he started giving his daughter morphine tablets the following April but denied giving her morphine for a seizure she suffered the day before her death.

    He said: 'At the time in a way the morphine wasn't on my prescription anymore. I only gave her one every eight or nine days. '

    Det Insp David Loughlin of Greater Manchester Police confirmed officers spoke to Mr and Mrs Murphy after reading the toxicology report. 

    He added: 'The parents agreed to go down to the station.

    'They were interviewed under caution and spoke to the police with regards to the drugs that they had been giving their daughter. The mother admitted that she had given her Tramadol and father that he had given her Tramadol and morphine.'
